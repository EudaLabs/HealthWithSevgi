# Chapter 1 — AIMS Scope, Context & Stakeholders

**ISO 42001 Reference:** Clause 4.1 (Internal/External Issues), Clause 4.2 (Interested Parties), Clause 4.3 (Scope), Clause 4.4 (AIMS)

This chapter establishes the foundation of our AI Management System by defining what our AI system is, who it affects, and what boundaries apply.

## 1.1 AI System Description

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **System Name** | HealthWithSevgi — ML Visualization Tool for Healthcare Professionals |
| **Purpose** | HealthWithSevgi is a browser-based educational tool that enables healthcare professionals and medical students to learn machine learning concepts through a guided 7-step wizard pipeline. The pipeline covers Clinical Context, Data Exploration, Data Preparation, Model Selection & Parameters, Results Evaluation, Explainability (SHAP), and Ethics & Bias Assessment, using real clinical datasets across 20 medical specialties. It does not make clinical decisions — it teaches ML literacy in a healthcare context. |
| **AI Techniques Used** | 8 supervised classification algorithms: K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), Support Vector Machine (SVM), Decision Tree, Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Naive Bayes, XGBoost, and LightGBM. Supporting techniques include SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) for class imbalance correction, SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) for model explainability with TreeExplainer, LinearExplainer, and KernelExplainer variants, and subgroup fairness analysis for bias detection across demographic groups. |
| **Deployment Context** | Browser-based Single Page Application (React 18 frontend + FastAPI backend), containerised via Docker for HuggingFace Spaces deployment. Designed exclusively for educational use with no clinical decision-making capability. All data processing occurs in-memory on the server with no persistent database. |
| **Lifecycle Stage** | Design, development, and educational deployment — NOT clinical production. The system lifecycle aligns with the SENG 430 course timeline (5 sprints, February–May 2026). No clinical validation, regulatory approval, or production hospital deployment is planned or intended. |

## 1.3 Internal & External Issues/Challenges

| Issue | Description & Relevance to AIMS |
|-------|--------------------------------|
| **External: Regulatory — EU AI Act** | The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) classifies healthcare AI systems as high-risk under Article 6 and Annex III. Although our tool is educational and not subject to regulatory enforcement, we demonstrate awareness of high-risk AI requirements throughout the pipeline. Step 7 includes an 8-item EU AI Act compliance checklist covering explainability, data documentation, bias auditing, human oversight, GDPR compliance, monitoring plans, incident reporting, and clinical validation requirements. This educational alignment ensures students understand real-world regulatory obligations. |
| **External: Regulatory — GDPR/KVKK** | The Turkish Personal Data Protection Law (KVKK, Law No. 6698) and the EU General Data Protection Regulation require data protection impact assessments for health data processing. Our tool mitigates regulatory risk by processing only published, de-identified datasets. No personal data is collected, stored, or transmitted. The in-memory session architecture with automatic eviction ensures no data persistence beyond the active session. If the tool were deployed with real patient data, a full DPIA, explicit patient consent (KVKK Article 6), and a designated Data Protection Officer would be required. |
| **External: Societal — Public concern about healthcare AI** | Growing public distrust of AI in clinical settings is fuelled by documented failures: racially biased pulse oximeters (2020), over-alerting sepsis prediction systems causing alert fatigue (2021), and dermatology AI underperforming on dark skin tones (2019). Our tool directly addresses this concern by including these three cases as educational case studies in Step 7, teaching users to critically evaluate AI systems rather than blindly trust them. |
| **External: Technological — Rapid evolution of XAI** | Explainable AI methods evolve rapidly (SHAP, LIME, counterfactual explanations, attention-based methods). Our choice of SHAP is well-established in the scientific literature and supported by multiple explainer types (TreeExplainer for tree-based models, LinearExplainer for logistic regression, KernelExplainer for KNN/SVM/NB). However, SHAP's computational cost for KernelExplainer can be significant, and newer methods may offer better trade-offs. The modular architecture of ExplainService allows future integration of alternative explainability methods. |
| **External: Market — Demand for AI-literate clinicians** | The WHO Global Strategy on Digital Health (2020–2025) and EU policy documents emphasise AI literacy for healthcare workers. Our tool directly serves this market need by covering 20 medical specialties with real clinical datasets, providing an accessible entry point for clinicians to understand ML concepts without requiring programming skills. |
| **Internal: Competence — Team ML/AI experience** | The development team consists of 5 software engineering students (SENG 430) with strong programming backgrounds but varying levels of ML/AI expertise. Roles span backend ML development (Berat Mert Gökkaya), frontend visualisation (Batuhan Bayazıt, Burak Aydoğmuş, Efe Çelik), and QA/documentation (Berfin Duru Alkan). This was mitigated by: (a) the structured 7-step wizard design that enforces a correct ML workflow, (b) use of well-established libraries (scikit-learn, XGBoost, LightGBM, SHAP) rather than custom implementations, and (c) iterative development across 5 sprints with instructor feedback at each gate. |
| **Internal: Infrastructure — No GPU, browser-based** | All ML computation runs on CPU via the FastAPI backend. This limits model training time and dataset size but is appropriate for the educational scope. Docker containerisation (python:3.12-slim base image) ensures reproducible deployment on HuggingFace Spaces. The in-memory architecture with OrderedDict-based LRU eviction (max 50 concurrent sessions and 50 trained models) manages resource constraints without external infrastructure dependencies. |
| **Internal: Timeline — 10-week sprint cycle** | The project follows a 5-sprint development cycle within one academic semester, with competing coursework demands. Risk of incomplete features was mitigated by progressive delivery: Sprint 1 (architecture + Steps 1–2), Sprint 2 (Steps 3–4), Sprint 3 (Steps 5–6 + bias detection), Sprint 4 (Step 7 + Docker + deployment), Sprint 5 (user testing + ISO report + final presentation). |
| **Internal: Resource — In-memory architecture** | The deliberate choice of an in-memory architecture with no external database simplifies privacy compliance (no persistent data to manage, encrypt, or delete) but limits concurrent user capacity. The MLService uses Python's `OrderedDict` with LRU eviction at 50 sessions and 50 models. The DataService uses an unbounded dictionary for session data. Under high concurrent load, older sessions are automatically evicted. This is acceptable for the educational deployment context. |

## 2.1 AI Policy Statement

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Policy Title & Version** | AI Policy for HealthWithSevgi ML Visualization Tool — Version 1.0, April 2026 |
| **Purpose** | This policy establishes the governance framework for responsible development and educational deployment of the HealthWithSevgi ML Visualization Tool. It ensures that all AI-related activities conducted by the development team adhere to principles of fairness, transparency, human oversight, privacy, safety, and accountability throughout the AI system lifecycle, in alignment with ISO/IEC 42001:2023 and the EU AI Act. |
| **Scope** | This policy governs all AI/ML components within the HealthWithSevgi system as defined in Section 1.2, including: 8 supervised classification algorithms and their hyperparameter tuning processes; the SHAP-based explainability engine (TreeExplainer, LinearExplainer, KernelExplainer); the subgroup fairness analysis and bias detection system; all 20 built-in clinical datasets and user-uploaded CSV processing; the 7-step wizard pipeline from clinical context definition through ethics assessment; and the Docker-containerised deployment on HuggingFace Spaces. This policy applies to all five team members across all five project sprints. |
| **Principle 1: Fairness** | We commit to ensuring equitable AI model performance across all patient demographic groups represented in our datasets. **Implementation:** Step 7 of our pipeline automatically conducts a subgroup fairness audit, evaluating model sensitivity (recall) across gender subgroups (Female and Male) and age bands (18–60, 61–75, 76+). A bias detection threshold of 10 percentage points triggers an automatic "review" status warning; a 20-point gap or sensitivity below 50% triggers an "action_needed" red alert with explicit text identifying the affected group and performance gap. Step 3 offers SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) to address training data class imbalance, with an adaptive k_neighbors formula: `max(1, min(5, min_class_count - 1))`. Three real-world AI failure case studies in Step 7 (pulse oximeter racial bias, sepsis alert over-alerting, dermatology dark skin underperformance) educate users about the consequences of unfair AI systems. |
| **Principle 2: Transparency** | We commit to making all AI model decisions interpretable and understandable to non-technical healthcare users. **Implementation:** Step 5 presents 7 evaluation metrics (accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, precision, F1-score, AUC-ROC, and Matthews Correlation Coefficient) with colour-coded thresholds (green ≥ 65%, orange 50–65%, red < 50%) and clinical-language explanations. Step 6 provides SHAP-based global feature importance showing the top contributing features with direction indicators (positive/negative/neutral influence on risk), and single-patient waterfall charts explaining individual predictions through the top 15 features. A clinical name mapping of 251 features translates raw variable names into clinician-friendly terms (e.g., "serum_creatinine" → "Serum Creatinine (mg/dL)"). Clinical sense-check notes accompany top features (e.g., "Elevated serum creatinine reflects impaired renal clearance, commonly co-occurs with heart failure"). |
| **Principle 3: Human Oversight** | We commit to ensuring that humans remain the ultimate decision-makers at all times. No automated clinical actions are taken by the tool. **Implementation:** Step 7 includes an EU AI Act compliance checklist item requiring user confirmation of a human oversight plan. Step 6 displays a prominent clinical disclaimer: "A model that cannot explain itself should not be trusted in clinical practice. Understanding why an AI made a prediction is essential before acting on it." The bottom of Step 6 states: "A clinician must always decide whether and how to act on any AI prediction. This tool is an educational aid, not a diagnostic device." All model outputs are presented as probability scores requiring active human interpretation — no automated referrals, alerts, or clinical actions are triggered. |
| **Principle 4: Privacy** | We commit to protecting patient data through a privacy-by-design architecture that minimises data collection and eliminates persistence. **Implementation:** All data processing occurs in-memory on the FastAPI backend server. The MLService uses Python OrderedDict-based LRU eviction at a maximum of 50 concurrent sessions and 50 trained models. No external database is used; no data is written to disk (except cached published datasets). The application footer displays: "Patient data is processed locally within this session. No patient data is stored or transmitted." CORS is restricted to development origins only (localhost:5173). The PDF certificate generated in Step 7 contains only aggregate performance metrics and fairness findings — no raw patient data. If the tool were deployed with real patient data, KVKK Article 6 explicit consent, GDPR Article 35 DPIA, encryption at rest/in transit, and a designated DPO would be required. |
| **Principle 5: Safety** | We commit to preventing harmful AI outputs through technical safeguards, performance thresholds, and user education. **Implementation — Technical safeguards:** The backend enforces input validation at the data ingestion boundary: datasets must have ≥10 rows, ≤50 MB file size, ≤20 unique target classes, and ≥2 columns (HTTP 422 rejection with specific error message on failure). Target columns where all values are identical are rejected during preparation because they would produce a degenerate model that always predicts one class. The SHAP explainability engine implements a multi-level fallback chain: if TreeExplainer fails, it falls back to LinearExplainer, then KernelExplainer, then permutation importance — ensuring that no model is presented to the user without at least a basic explanation of its decision factors. The `predict_proba` method has a fallback to `decision_function` with sigmoid transformation, and ultimately returns a default [0.5, 0.5] prediction rather than raising an unhandled exception. **Implementation — Performance thresholds:** Step 5 triggers a low sensitivity warning (red alert) when model sensitivity falls below 50%, indicating the model may miss clinically important cases. An overfitting warning triggers when the train-test accuracy gap exceeds 10 percentage points. **Implementation — User education:** The educational-only scope is declared in the application footer, Step 1 warning cards ("ML cannot replace clinical judgment or doctor-patient relationships"), and throughout the ISO documentation. The model comparison feature encourages validation across multiple algorithms rather than relying on a single model. Step 1 explicitly states: "This is a learning tool — experiment freely with different models. No real patient data leaves your browser session." |
| **Principle 6: Accountability** | We commit to clear assignment of responsibility for all AI governance activities within the project. **Implementation:** Governance roles are formally mapped in Section 2.2, assigning each team member to specific ISO 42001 AI governance responsibilities. The sprint gate process (5 gates across 5 sprints) serves as management review checkpoints aligned with ISO 42001 Clause 9.3. This ISO 42001 compliance report documents all governance decisions with traceability to specific source code files, functions, and pipeline steps. Git version control (101+ commits across 5 team members) provides a complete audit trail of all development decisions. The PDCA cycle is documented through the two-phase report submission (Plan: Ch. 1–3 first submission; Do: implementation; Check: instructor feedback + peer audit; Act: corrective actions in final submission). |
| **Compliance Commitments** | ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (AI Management System — primary compliance framework for this report); EU AI Act Regulation 2024/1689 (educational alignment with high-risk AI requirements in Article 6, Annex III); ISO/IEC 25059 (AI system quality model — quality metrics in Step 5); KVKK Law No. 6698 / EU GDPR (data protection alignment through privacy-by-design architecture). |
| **Review Schedule** | This policy shall be reviewed: (1) at project completion (May 2026) as part of the final ISO 42001 submission; (2) when any Step 7 bias audit detects a sensitivity gap reaching or exceeding 20 percentage points ("action_needed" status), indicating a governance-level fairness concern; (3) when the AIMS scope changes (e.g., addition of new specialties, models, or deployment targets); (4) when relevant regulations are updated (e.g., EU AI Act enforcement timeline, KVKK amendments). |

## 3.2 Data Quality & Validation

| Topic | Documentation |
|-------|--------------|
| **Quality Criteria** | Our tool enforces the following acceptance criteria for all datasets (both built-in and user-uploaded), implemented in `DataService` (`backend/app/services/data_service.py`): |
| | **Minimum rows:** ≥10 patients (constant `MIN_ROWS = 10`). Datasets below this threshold are rejected because cross-validation requires a minimum number of samples per fold, and stratified splitting with fewer than 10 samples risks having empty folds. |
| | **Required columns:** ≥2 (at least 1 feature column + 1 target column). The target variable must be explicitly selected by the user via the Column Mapper in Step 2. |
| | **File format:** `.csv` only. This constraint ensures consistent parsing with `pandas.read_csv()` and avoids encoding ambiguities of Excel formats. Built-in datasets from UCI that arrive as ARFF or Excel are pre-converted to CSV during the dataset loading pipeline. |
| | **Maximum file size:** ≤50 MB (constant `MAX_UPLOAD_MB = 50`). This limit prevents memory exhaustion on the containerised deployment (HuggingFace Spaces has limited RAM). |
| | **Maximum target classes:** ≤20 unique values (constant `MAX_TARGET_CLASSES = 20`). Targets with more than 20 classes are rejected as likely continuous variables misidentified as categorical, which would produce meaningless classification results. |
| | **Imbalance detection:** An automatic warning is triggered when the ratio of the largest to smallest class exceeds 1.5:1 (constant `IMBALANCE_RATIO_THRESHOLD = 1.5`). This alert in Step 2 prompts the user to consider enabling SMOTE in Step 3. |
| | **Validation behaviour on failure:** When any criterion is violated, the backend returns an HTTP 422 (Unprocessable Entity) response with a specific error message identifying the violated constraint. The frontend displays this as an inline error alert in the Step 2 Data Exploration interface, allowing the user to correct the issue (e.g., upload a different file, select a valid target column). |
| **Missing Value Handling** | Three strategies are available in Step 3, selectable by the user via `PrepSettings.missing_strategy`: |
| | **Median imputation** (default: `missing_strategy = "median"`): Recommended for continuous clinical measurements such as blood pressure, serum creatinine, and glucose levels. The median is robust to outliers common in clinical data (e.g., extreme creatinine values in renal failure patients). Implementation: training set median computed via `train_df.median()`, then applied to both training and test sets to prevent data leakage. Clinically appropriate because it preserves the central tendency of the measurement distribution without being skewed by pathological extremes. |
| | **Mode imputation** (`missing_strategy = "mode"`): Recommended for categorical variables such as gender, smoking status, and diagnostic codes, where the most frequent value represents the typical case. Also appropriate for ordinal scales with limited values (e.g., GOLD COPD stage). Implementation: `train_df.mode().iloc[0]` computed from training set only, with fallback to median if mode is empty. Clinically appropriate because categorical variables have no meaningful "middle" value. |
| | **Drop rows** (`missing_strategy = "drop"`): Recommended only when missingness is random (MCAR — Missing Completely At Random) and the dataset is large enough to absorb the sample loss without introducing selection bias. **Clinical risk:** If missingness is systematic (e.g., lab tests not ordered for low-risk patients — MAR/MNAR), dropping rows biases the remaining data toward sicker patients who received more comprehensive testing. This can inflate model sensitivity but reduce generalisability to the broader patient population. Applied before train/test split on the full dataset, then reapplied within each split. |
| **Normalisation** | Three methods available via `PrepSettings.normalization`: |
| | **Z-score standardisation** (default: `normalization = "zscore"`): Implemented via `sklearn.preprocessing.StandardScaler`. Transforms features to zero mean and unit variance. Recommended when features have different units and scales (e.g., combining age in years with creatinine in mg/dL with platelet count in 10^9/L). Essential for distance-based algorithms (KNN, SVM) where feature magnitude directly affects distance calculations. Maps to standard deviations from the mean — a concept familiar to clinicians who routinely interpret lab results using reference ranges and standard deviations. Scaler is fitted on training data only and applied to both sets to prevent data leakage. |
| | **Min-Max scaling** (`normalization = "minmax"`): Implemented via `sklearn.preprocessing.MinMaxScaler`. Scales all features to the [0, 1] range. Recommended when the original value range is bounded and meaningful (e.g., satisfaction scores, percentage-based features). Preserves zero values and relative distances within the original range. Less robust to outliers than z-score because a single extreme value compresses all other values into a narrow band. |
| | **None** (`normalization = "none"`): No scaling applied. Acceptable for tree-based models (Decision Tree, Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM) which are inherently scale-invariant because they make split decisions based on feature thresholds, not distances. **Risk:** KNN and SVM will be dominated by high-magnitude features (e.g., platelet count ~250,000 vs. age ~65) if normalisation is skipped, producing clinically meaningless distance calculations. |
| **Class Imbalance — SMOTE** | Implemented via `imblearn.over_sampling.SMOTE` in `DataService.prepare_data()`: |
| | **What it does:** SMOTE generates synthetic minority-class samples by interpolating between existing minority-class instances in feature space. For each minority sample, it identifies its k nearest minority-class neighbours and creates new synthetic samples along the line segments connecting them. |
| | **Why it is needed in healthcare:** Clinical datasets frequently exhibit severe class imbalance because the conditions being predicted (sepsis, stroke, cancer, mortality) are inherently rare events. Without SMOTE, models optimise for majority class accuracy and achieve deceptively high overall accuracy (e.g., 95%) while completely failing to detect the clinically important minority class (e.g., 0% sensitivity for sepsis). In healthcare, missing a true positive (false negative) is often far more dangerous than a false alarm (false positive). |
| | **Adaptive k_neighbors:** The implementation uses `k_neighbors = max(1, min(5, min_class_count - 1))` to handle very small minority classes gracefully. When the minority class has only 2–3 samples, k is reduced accordingly rather than failing. Random state is fixed at 42 for reproducibility. |
| | **Why applied to training data only:** Applying SMOTE to the test set would inflate performance metrics by testing on artificial data that statistically resembles the training distribution. The test set must reflect the real-world class distribution to provide honest performance estimates. This is a fundamental ML evaluation principle (ISO 42001 Annex A.6.4 — Data Preparation). |
| | **How Step 3 displays it:** The Step 3 preparation response includes `class_distribution_before` and `class_distribution_after` dictionaries, allowing the frontend to display a before/after class distribution comparison chart showing original versus SMOTE-balanced class counts. |

## 3.3 Data Privacy & Provenance

| Topic | Documentation |
|-------|--------------|
| **Privacy Architecture** | **In-memory processing with no persistence:** User-uploaded CSV files are parsed by the FastAPI backend into pandas DataFrames held entirely in server memory. No data is written to disk, no external database is used, and no user data is transmitted to any third-party service. |
| | **Session-only data lifecycle:** Each user session is assigned a UUID. The `MLService` stores session data (preprocessed DataFrames, trained models, SHAP values, comparison results) in `OrderedDict`-based stores with LRU (Least Recently Used) eviction. Maximum capacity: 50 concurrent sessions and 50 trained models. When the 51st session or model is created, the oldest entry is automatically evicted. No manual deletion mechanism is needed — all data is ephemeral. |
| | **Privacy notice:** The application footer displays the following persistent privacy statement: *"Patient data is processed locally within this session. No patient data is stored or transmitted."* Additionally, Step 1 displays a green information card: *"No real patient data leaves your browser session."* |
| | **CORS restriction:** Cross-Origin Resource Sharing is restricted to `http://localhost:5173` and `http://127.0.0.1:5173` (frontend development server only). In the Docker production deployment, the frontend static files are served by the same process (`main_hf.py`), eliminating cross-origin requests entirely. |
| | **Certificate data exclusion:** The PDF certificate generated in Step 7 (via `CertificateService`) contains only: model type, aggregate performance metrics, confusion matrix summary, cross-validation statistics, subgroup fairness findings, and EU AI Act checklist status. No raw patient data, individual patient records, or feature values are included in the certificate. The certificate footer displays a prominent disclaimer: *"This certificate confirms completion of an educational exercise only. The AI model described herein is NOT validated for clinical use."* |
| | **What would need to change for real patient data under KVKK/GDPR:** (1) Explicit informed consent mechanism compliant with KVKK Article 5/6 and GDPR Article 6-9 for processing special category health data; (2) Full Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) per GDPR Article 35 and KVKK Board decisions; (3) Encryption at rest (AES-256) and in transit (TLS 1.3); (4) Right-to-deletion endpoint with cryptographic data destruction verification; (5) Appointed Data Protection Officer (DPO) per GDPR Article 37; (6) Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with any third-party infrastructure providers; (7) Audit trail for all data access with immutable logging; (8) Regular data protection audits per KVKK Article 12. |
| **Provenance Tracking** | **Built-in datasets (20 specialties):** Each dataset's source is documented in the specialty registry (`backend/app/services/specialty_registry.py`) with: original repository URL (UCI, Kaggle, PhysioNet, Harvard Dataverse), dataset description, clinical context, and license information. Step 2 displays the dataset description and source attribution in the Data Exploration interface. The complete provenance inventory is documented in Section 3.1 above. |
| | **User-uploaded data:** For user-uploaded CSV files, the tool captures: filename, upload timestamp (implicit in session creation), row count, column count, column data types, missing value counts, and class distribution. No persistent lineage record is maintained beyond the session lifetime because the educational context does not require long-term provenance tracking. |
| | **What a clinical deployment would need:** Automated data lineage tracking system (e.g., Apache Atlas, OpenLineage); immutable provenance records with chain-of-custody documentation per KVKK Article 12; version control for dataset updates; data quality monitoring with automated alerts for distribution drift; source-to-prediction traceability for regulatory audit. |
| **Bias in Data** | **Class imbalance across datasets:** Multiple datasets exhibit significant class imbalance that could bias model predictions: Stroke Prediction (<5% positive), ICU/Sepsis (~10% positive), Cervical Cancer (<10% positive biopsy), Obstetrics/CTG (~8% pathological). Step 2 automatically detects imbalance ratios >1.5:1 and displays a warning. Step 3 offers SMOTE to address training data imbalance. |
| | **Demographic representation gaps:** The Pima Indians Diabetes dataset contains exclusively Pima Indian women ≥21 years — one of the highest diabetes prevalence populations globally — severely limiting generalisability. The Indian Liver Patient Dataset is 75% male and geographically limited to Andhra Pradesh, India. The HAM10000 dermatology dataset is predominantly light-skinned patients from Austrian and Australian clinics. Several datasets (Heart Failure, CKD, Ophthalmology) are single-institution studies with inherent geographic and demographic biases. |
| | **Historical treatment disparities:** Datasets collected from clinical practice may encode historical disparities in care delivery. For example, missing lab values in the Sepsis dataset may reflect under-testing of patients perceived as low-risk, which could correlate with demographic factors. Treatment patterns in the Pharmacy Readmission dataset (1999–2008) reflect outdated prescribing practices. |
| | **Step 7 training data representation analysis:** Step 7 computes and displays the gender distribution in the training dataset versus general population norms (50%/50% reference) using a grouped bar chart. Age group distributions are analysed across 18–60, 61–75, and 76+ bands. This allows users to visually assess whether the training data adequately represents the target patient population. When representation is significantly skewed (e.g., <30% of a gender), the subgroup performance table in Step 7 provides per-group sensitivity, specificity, and F1 scores, enabling the user to determine whether the representation gap translates into a performance gap. The tool does not automatically correct representation imbalance (SMOTE addresses class imbalance, not demographic imbalance), but the visual comparison against population norms empowers users to make informed judgments about the generalisability of their model to the broader patient population. |
